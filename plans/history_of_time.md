# A short history of date and time types in programming languages

*What and when was the first computer language to define a date and/or time type? How, historically, did we end up with separate types for dates, times, and datetime (with zone and naive)?*

Short version: it depends entirely on what you count as a *type*, and the honest answer splits three ways. If you mean date-shaped **data**, COBOL (1960). If you mean a first-class **broken-down/scalar time type** in the type system, Unix C's `<time.h>` (`time_t` + `struct tm`, 1970s). If you mean the named **civil type family** (DATE / TIME / TIMESTAMP) we'd recognise today, the database world — Oracle's `DATE` (1979), standardised in **SQL-92** (1992). The interesting part is *why* those are three different answers, because that history is exactly how we ended up with the separate-types mess.

## "Read the clock" came decades before "a date type"

Every early system could *read* a real-time clock, and the business languages could fetch the current date as a runtime service long before any of them modelled a date as a distinct type. FORTRAN (1957) and ALGOL (1958/60) had no concept of time at all. FLOW-MATIC (Grace Hopper, ~1955–59) and its descendant COBOL (1960) were business-data languages that *worked with* dates constantly — but as ordinary numeric fields. A COBOL date was `PIC 9(6)`, a six-digit number holding `YYMMDD`, and `ACCEPT date-field FROM DATE` dropped today's value into it. There was no `DATE` category in the PICTURE clause; the type system did not know a date from an inventory count.

That absence is not a footnote — it *is* the Y2K story. Because dates were just numbers chosen to be as small as storage allowed, the century was thrown away, and there was no type to enforce otherwise. The reflexive answer "COBOL had dates" is instructive precisely because it's wrong in the way that mattered: it had date-shaped data, not a date type. (COBOL didn't get genuine first-class temporal types until the 2002/2014 standards; RPG got them in RPG IV, ~1994 — both mirroring SQL.)

Three languages of the same era show the pattern from different angles. **BCPL** (Richards, 1967) *couldn't* have a date type: it is typeless, with exactly one data type — the machine *word* — so meaning comes from the operator applied, not the value, and any date handling is library routines over words. **PL/I** (IBM, 1964–66) had no date type but shipped the *functions* early — a `DATE` built-in returning `CHARACTER(6)` as `YYMMDD` (a Y2K liability for COBOL's exact reason) and `TIME` returning `CHARACTER(9)` as `HHMMSSttt` (to the millisecond); only Enterprise PL/I (~2000s) added a Y2K-driven `DATE` *attribute* on variables, type-adjacent but not a first-class type. **ALGOL** blessed one at neither end of its life: ALGOL 60 had only `integer`/`real`/`Boolean` and no standard I/O at all, while ALGOL 68's rich `struct`/`mode` system let you *build* a date structure but never provided a standard one.

## The machine underneath: when a program could actually read the date

There is a substrate question under those language features: a `DATE` built-in or `ACCEPT FROM DATE` is only as real as the machine's ability to *tell* the program what day it is — and that arrived later than the syntax, in a revealing order. "Real-time" computing came first but is a red herring here: **Whirlwind** (MIT, ~1951), called the first real-time computer, meant *responding to inputs as they happen* (flight simulation, then air defence), not *having a readable calendar clock* — and it was a research/military machine, not a commercial one. Early "real-time clocks" on commercial machines were **interval timers**: a hardware counter ticking at a fixed rate (often the 50/60 Hz line frequency) that software *counted* to keep the time of day from an operator-set starting value. There was no calendar chip.

The first clean case of a commercial machine whose *programming language* could read the current date is the **IBM System/360** era. The architecture (1964) defined an interval timer at real storage location 80 (X'50'), and **OS/360** (1966) counted it to maintain the time of day *and calendar date*, handing both to programs through the `TIME` service (SVC 11) as packed-decimal Julian `YYDDD` — exactly what a COBOL `ACCEPT FROM DATE` / `FROM DAY` compiled into. But the date was not read from a clock: the **operator keyed it in at system startup**, and the OS advanced it by counting ticks. For years the date a program read was only as trustworthy as what a human typed at boot — the same "date as fragile human-entered data" that produced `PIC 9(6)` and Y2K.

A genuine **hardware** clock came only with **System/370** (1970): the Time-of-Day (TOD) clock, a 64-bit counter with a fixed epoch of **1900-01-01**, read by one instruction (`STCK`, Store Clock). It is the direct hardware ancestor of what Unix would soon package as `time_t` — a monotonic count from an epoch that software projects into a civil date. So the pieces landed in a telling order: the COBOL verb was standardised in 1968, the OS *service* it read existed from ~1966, and the *hardware clock* that now backs it appeared only in 1970. The language could ask for the date before the machine had a real clock to give it.

## The deepest split was born in Unix: instant vs. broken-down civil time

The single most consequential design decision is the one Unix `<time.h>` crystallised in the 1970s: **two representations for the same moment.**

* `time_t` — an arithmetic scalar, seconds since the 1970-01-01 UTC epoch. This is *the instant*: a point on the physical timeline, unambiguous, cheap to compare and subtract.

* `struct tm` — the same moment exploded into year/month/day/hour/min/sec fields. This is *civil time*: what a human reads off a wall clock and a calendar.

And critically, the conversion between them (`gmtime` vs `localtime`, `mktime`) **requires a timezone**. `gmtime` projects the instant into UTC; `localtime` consults the zone database and gives you a different set of civil fields for the same instant. That is the origin of nearly everything downstream: the instant is zone-free and absolute, but the moment you want a human-readable projection, a zone has to enter, and one instant yields many civil readings. Note the asymmetry that still trips people up — `time_t` is never ambiguous; *all* the ambiguity lives in the civil projection.

## The named family came from databases, standardised in SQL-92

The types we actually name today — `DATE`, `TIME`, `TIMESTAMP`, `INTERVAL`, and the `WITH`/`WITHOUT TIME ZONE` variants — grew up in the relational world because a database *column* needs a declared type with validation and formatting, not a raw integer. Tellingly, SQL's *own* birthplace — IBM's System R (1974–79) — had no date type: its example queries filter a `CHARACTER(6)` `YYMMDD` column with string literals (`DATE BETWEEN '750000' AND '751231'`, where `'750000'` is not a real date but a string chosen to sort below all of 1975), so the named type family postdates the language that now hosts it. Oracle shipped a `DATE` type in its first commercial SQL release (v2, 1979), storing date-plus-time to the second; Ingres and the 1970s 4GLs (NOMAD, RAMIS, FOCUS) were in the same territory. But the original SQL standard (SQL-86/89) had **no** datetime types at all — they were vendor extensions until **SQL-92** pulled the whole family into the standard: `DATE`, `TIME`, `TIMESTAMP`, `TIMESTAMP WITH TIME ZONE`, and `INTERVAL` (itself split into year-month vs day-time). Essentially every modern language's date library recapitulates the SQL-92 taxonomy. On the general-purpose-language side, Ada's standard `Calendar` package (1983) with its private `Time` type is the notable early first-class temporal type outside a database.

## Why the types kept subdividing — the axes that forced each split

Once you accept "instant vs. civil," the rest is a series of forced separations, each because the two things being merged genuinely behave differently:

**Date vs. time vs. datetime** — different civil *domains*, not just granularities. A birthday or an invoice due date is a *day* (`2026-07-04`); asking "at what time?" is a category error, and stamping it `00:00:00` is a lie that immediately raises "midnight in which zone?". A shop's opening time or a daily alarm (`07:30`) is a point on the 24-hour dial with no particular day. A log entry is a specific civil moment. Three domains, three types — SQL-92's `DATE` / `TIME` / `TIMESTAMP`.

**Naive vs. aware (the "with/without time zone" split)** — the subtle one. A civil reading like `2026-07-04 15:00` is *not an instant* until a zone pins it. And sometimes you deliberately want it to stay unpinned: an alarm set for "07:00" must ring at 07:00 *wherever the device is and across DST* — pin it to a UTC instant and it fires at the wrong wall-clock time forever after. Other times you mean one specific moment — "launch at 2026-07-04 15:00 New York" — which *is* a single instant, convertible to any zone. These are different intentions, so they became different types: SQL's `WITHOUT`/`WITH TIME ZONE`, Python's naive vs. aware `datetime` (the `tzinfo`-carrying distinction, added in the `datetime` module in Python 2.3, 2003), Java's `LocalDateTime` vs. `ZonedDateTime`. The classic production bug — storing a naive local time as if it were UTC — is exactly what having *separate types* is meant to make unrepresentable.

**Offset vs. zone** — a further refinement of "aware." `+05:00` is a fixed number; `America/New_York` is a *rule set* with DST transitions and historical changes. You need the rule set to do correct future arithmetic ("9 AM next November" must survive a DST shift); a bare offset is only enough to re-pin a past instant. This distinction only became tractable because of the **IANA/Olson timezone database** (Arthur David Olson, ~1986) — the shared infrastructure that made "zone" a first-class concept distinct from "offset." Hence Java's `OffsetDateTime` vs. `ZonedDateTime`.

**Duration vs. period** — the orthogonal *length* axis has the same fault line. "3600 seconds" is a physical quantity (`Duration`); "1 month" or "1 year" is a *calendar* quantity (`Period` / SQL `INTERVAL YEAR TO MONTH`) whose length in seconds depends on *when* you apply it, because months vary, DST days are 23 or 25 hours, and leap seconds exist. Civil arithmetic is non-uniform, so elapsed time also had to split into two types.

**java.time (Java 8, 2014)** — Stephen Colebourne's JSR-310, evolved from his Joda-Time (~2005) — is the fullest crystallisation of the whole taxonomy, and it's worth reading as the "final form" of this history: `Instant` (the machine timeline), `LocalDate`/`LocalTime`/`LocalDateTime` (naive civil), `OffsetDateTime`/`ZonedDateTime` (aware), `Duration` (machine-scale) vs. `Period` (calendar-scale). Every one of those types exists to keep two things that behave differently from silently coercing into each other.

## Calendar applications forced the next layer: recurrence and intervals

One more source tells you when date/time capability had truly matured: calendar *applications*. Two different things wear the name. **Calendrical computation** — printing a calendar, computing a weekday, calculating Easter (the original "computus") — needs only date *arithmetic*, never asks the machine what today is, is as old as computing (Unix's `cal` is its survivor), and so proves nothing about clocks. **Calendaring and scheduling** — a diary, appointment book, meeting scheduler — is the real forcing function: it must know "today," alert relative to now, and store durable dated records.

That second kind tracks the TOD services almost exactly. Rudimentary scheduling ran on 1960s time-sharing systems, but the recognisable electronic calendar appears in the **mid-1970s** — the first electronic-calendar patent is credited to Alfred B. Levine (filed 1975), and time-sharing systems shipped reminder tools (Unix's `calendar(1)`, 1979) — precisely once operating systems reliably kept the date. Group calendaring became a product with **IBM PROFS** (developed late 1970s, released 1981): shared calendars plus email on mainframes, the usual answer to "first production group-scheduling system." Personal computers caught up by 1983–84 (the Apple Lisa's Calendar desk accessory; Borland SideKick's pop-up appointment book).

The causality runs both ways, and that is the point. Basic TOD *enabled* basic calendars in the 1970s, but the *ambitions* of calendaring — recurring events, time-zone-aware scheduling across sites, conflict detection, free/busy — then *drove* richer date/time capability than the bare types offered. That demand culminates in **iCalendar** (RFC 2445, 1998; RFC 5545, 2009), the standard event-and-recurrence model, whose `RRULE` recurrence rules and interval semantics are exactly what Tempo implements today (`Tempo.RRule`, the iCal integration).

## The throughline to Tempo

The reason Tempo exists is that this whole lineage inherited one framing from `struct tm`: a civil value is an **instant** (possibly incomplete, possibly ambiguous). That framing is what forces the naive/aware coercion, the invented `00:00:00`, the "which midnight" question, and the runtime-invalid date. Tempo's move — treating `2026` or `2026-07` as a **bounded interval** (a whole year, a whole month) with a half-open `[from, to)` span rather than an uncertain point — dissolves several of these historical knots at once: a partial value is not an under-specified instant needing a zone and a zero-time, it's a *span*, and spans compose, compare (Allen), and never denote an invalid date. In a real sense Tempo is a rejection of the specific 1970s decision — "a date is a broken-down instant" — that everything above was built on top of.
